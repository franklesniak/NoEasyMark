# Introduction

## Intent, Purpose and Scope

The DER Interconnection Guidelines for Customers ("the Guidelines") provide guidance applicable to Interconnection Customers ("IC") planning to interconnect and operate Distributed Energy Resources ("DER"), including generators, in parallel with the ComEd distribution system. The Guidelines are intended to provide standards that apply to various aspects of Generating Interconnection Customers' interfacing with ComEd.

This document addresses the Interconnection Customer's responsibilities within the DER interconnection process, regulatory and general system requirements, including relay and protection, SCADA, metering, and telemetry, as well as an overview of the process itself.

The Guidelines supersede previous ComEd interconnection guideline documents, including the ComEd document titled "DER Interconnection Guidelines for Customers Interconnection for Parallel Generation" dated July 20th, 2018.

The interconnection of Distributed Energy Resources is governed by the Illinois Administrative Code in Title 83, Part 466, "Electric Interconnection of Distributed Generation Facilities" ("Part 466") for DER facilities equal to or less than 10 MVA; and Part 467, "Electric Interconnection of Large Distributed Generation Facilities" ("Part 467") for DER facilities greater than 10 MVA (collectively, the "interconnection rules").

## Responsibilities of the Interconnection Customer

### Adherence to Contracts and Agreements

Interconnection Customers must adhere to all contracts and agreements throughout the `ComEd System DER Interconnection Process`. Those terms can be found within the contract sections of `Part 466, Appendix D` and `Part 467, Appendix C`.

### Customer DER Equipment

The Interconnection Customer is responsible for designing, installing, operating, and maintaining its own equipment in accordance with IEEE Standard 1547, Good Utility Practice(s), the National Electrical Code, the National Electrical Safety Code, North America Electric Reliability Council, OSHA, any applicable independent system operator standards, ComEd planning criteria and guidelines, and all applicable laws, statutes, guidelines and regulations, including those specified in the attached appendices. This includes installing, setting, and maintaining all protective devices necessary to protect the Interconnection Customer's facilities. The requirements specified in the Guidelines are designed to protect ComEd facilities and customers and to maintain distribution system safety and reliability. These guidelines are not designed for the protection of Interconnection Customer facilities.

ComEd is responsible for identifying the equipment to which the interconnection customer may interconnect to the Electric Distribution Company (EDC). The Interconnection Customer provides the geographic coordinates for the location of the Interconnection Customer's P.O.I.

### Interconnection Costs

Pursuant to the interconnection rules, the Interconnection Customer is responsible for the costs of interconnection facilities and distribution upgrades resulting from the interconnection of the customer's DER facility with ComEd's distribution system. The interconnection review process identifies adverse system impacts and the corresponding interconnection facilities and distribution upgrades required to mitigate those impacts. Such interconnection facilities and distribution upgrades may include, but are not limited to:

- Relay and protection equipment
- Distribution automation equipment
- Telemetry
- Protective relay setting changes
- Metering upgrades

Relocation, upgrades, or installation of other ComEd facilities. The information provided in [Appendix 7: DER Interconnection Cost Guide](21-appendix-7-cost-guide.md), is intended to provide general cost guidance for commonly identified scopes of interconnection facilities and distribution upgrades and is aligned with ComEd's engineering and design standards. The scope of work and associated costs for individual interconnection projects can vary significantly and may be revised as location-specific conditions are identified during interconnection studies and throughout all phases of the project, including, the design, procurement, and construction of interconnection facilities and distribution upgrades associated with each project.

### Protection Requirements Related to DER Interconnection

The ComEd distribution system includes facilities owned, controlled, and operated by ComEd, is constantly changing due to shifts in loading and the addition and removal of generation. Future changes in the ComEd system may require that existing customer protection facilities or settings for a DER facility be upgraded or modified. It is the responsibility of the DER customer to implement the necessary changes to meet the ComEd requirements.

## Types of Generation Facilities

There are two main categories of non-utility generation facilities that operate in parallel with the ComEd system:

Qualifying Facility (QF) is a generation facility that meets requirements specified by federal law under the Public Utility Regulatory Policy Act of 1978 (PURPA). There are two general classifications that meet these requirements:

- Co-generation facilities which sequentially produce electric energy and another form of energy, such as heat or steam, using the same fuel source.
- Small Power Production facilities which use biomass, waste, renewable resources (water, wind or solar), Energy Storage System (ESS) or geothermal as a primary energy source.

Certification requirements for both classifications of QFs cover various facility aspects including generation technology, fuel sources, operating efficiency, and ownership, and are established by The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) under CFR, Title 18, Part 292, "Regulations under sections 201 and 210 of PURPA with regard to small power production and co-generation."

A Non-Qualifying Facility is a generation facility that does not qualify under the provisions of PURPA, such as the typical merchant power plants that sell their output on the wholesale market or enter into Power Purchase Agreements with a local utility or industrial customer. Independent Power Producers frequently fall into this category.
